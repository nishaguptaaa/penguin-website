Did You Know? About Emperor Penguins!

Emperor penguins (Aptenodytes forsteri) are the largest of all penguin species. They stand nearly four feet tall and weigh up to 88 pounds. They are native to Antarctica and are uniquely adapted to survive one of the harshest environments on Earth. With thick layers of blubber and densely packed feathers, emperor penguins can withstand freezing temperatures as low as -60°F (-51°C). Their tightly packed feathers provide waterproofing and insulation, preventing heat loss in extreme cold. Unlike most birds, they do not build nests; instead, males balance a single egg on their feet, covering it with a warm brood pouch for incubation. During this two-month period, males fast, relying on stored fat reserves while enduring brutal winter storms with winds exceeding 120 mph (193 km/h). During this time, females travel up to 50 miles to the open ocean to hunt and return with regurgitated food for their newly hatched chicks. Emperor penguins are exceptional divers, capable of reaching depths over 1,800 feet and holding their breath for more than 20 minutes in search of fish, squid, and krill. Their tightly knit colonies provide warmth and protection from the cold, as they take turns rotating between the outer and inner sections of the huddle. They are highly social creatures!

Here are some more fun facts!

  • Emperor penguins have the highest feather density of any bird, with about 100 feathers per square inch, helping to trap heat.
  • They can swim up to 5.6 mph (9 km/h), making them agile hunters in the water.
  • Each penguin has a distinct vocalization, allowing mates and chicks to recognize them in massive colonies.
  • They can reduce their heart rate to as low as 15 beats per minute to conserve oxygen while diving.
  • If a chick's parents don't return, other adult penguins may adopt and care for the orphaned chick.
